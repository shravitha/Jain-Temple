Jainism

Jainism also known as Jain Dharma, is an Indian religion. Jainism traces its spiritual ideas and history through the succession of twenty-four tirthankars (supreme preachers of Dharma), with the first in the current time cycle being Rishabhadeva, whom the tradition holds to have lived millions of years ago, the twenty-third tirthankara Parshvanatha, whom historians date to the 9th century BCE, and the twenty-fourth tirthankara Mahavira, around 600 BCE. Jainism is considered to be an eternal dharma with the tirthankaras guiding every time cycle of the cosmology. The three main pillars of Jainism are ahiṃsā (non-violence), anekāntavāda (non-absolutism), and aparigraha (asceticism).

Jain monks take five main vows: ahiṃsā (non-violence), satya (truth), asteya (not stealing), brahmacharya (chastity), and aparigraha (non-possessiveness). These principles have affected Jain culture in many ways, such as leading to a predominantly lacto-vegetarian lifestyle. Parasparopagraho jīvānām (the function of souls is to help one another) is the faith's motto, and the Ṇavkar mantra is its most common and strongest prayer.

Jainism in Moodabidre

The Jains inhabited the region in and around Moodabidri as early as 3 centuries B.C. During this period, a group of Monks led by Shrutha Kevali Bhadrabahu from northern India visited Shravanabelagola. While fossils from before the 7th century A.D. aren't accessible, evidence from ancient relics tied to Jain heritage scattered across various parts of the D.K. District supports this assertion. The significant religious influence of Bhagawan Parshwanatha on this area is evident. Historians suggest that Bhagavan Mahaveer's "SAMAVASARANA" (Dharmashala Mantapa) journeyed through places like Kerala and Mysore (Hemanga) via Thulunadu. During Bhagavan Mahaveera's era, this district earned the title "Sathyaputhras" (Blessed Sons).

Around the 3rd-4th century B.C., Shruthakevali Bhadrabahu Muni, along with Mourya Chakravarthi Chandragupta and 12,000 disciples, journeyed south and settled in Shravanabelagola. Some of his disciples spread to Tamil Nadu, Telugu regions, Karnataka, and Thowlava, teaching and promoting religious principles. Many of these Jains constructed Basadis, engaging in religious activities or trade. A few ventured to islands, amassing great wealth and becoming prominent figures, while others focused on studying ancient texts like Dwadashanga and delving into Vedas, literature, and religious pursuits

Those who performed daily rituals, worship, and poojas in Basadis, adhering to set principles under religious leadership, were referred to as priests or purohits. (Such Jains carrying out daily Archana, Pooja, and worship with reverence, including Abhishekha to Jineshwara, were known as 'Indras' in the undivided South Kanara district.)
Challenging times led to a decline in the Jain population in Moodabidri, previously known for its prosperity. Consequently, Jain temples fell into disrepair, and thick bamboo jungles grew unchecked. By the beginning of the 8th century, a monk from Shravanabelagola stumbled upon the area. While searching for a place to relieve himself, he witnessed an unusual sight in a corner where Guru Basadi now stands: a cow and a tiger affectionately grooming each other and sharing water from the same bowl. This extraordinary interaction between natural enemies led the monk to believe the place held a unique sanctity. Clearing the dense bamboo, the monk discovered an 11-foot-tall, captivating statue of Sri Bhagawan Sri Parshwanatha Swamy, crafted from black stone and standing tall. The statue, dating back a thousand years, prompted the construction of a splendid new Basadi to house it, as guided by the monk.
Installed by the aforementioned monk known as "Shramana Guru," the Basadi earned the name "Guru Basadi." Subsequently, numerous Jain Shravakas were initiated according to religious rituals and sent across the state to propagate these principles, emphasizing nonviolence for societal and national peace.
"Guru Basadi," also referred to as "Siddhantha Basadi," houses preserved ancient religious texts like "Moolaagama" and "Parama Moolaagama," known as 'DHAVALAS,' printed in old Kannada. Moreover, devotees can behold ancient statues, as old as 1000 years, carved meticulously from precious gemstones. The revered statues were presented to this Basadi for safekeeping by order of the religious leader. These treasures stand as unique instances of pen-written texts, distinct from the needle-inscribed and inked method employed in North India. Among thousands of discovered religious volumes, only these three Dhawalas are documented in this style.

About

Saavira Kambada Temple, also known as the Chandranatha Temple, is a renowned Jain temple in Moodabidri, Karnataka, India. It boasts a remarkable feature of 1000 pillars, making it a significant architectural marvel.
